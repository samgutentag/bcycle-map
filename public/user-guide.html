1. What this is

This is a live map of a city's BCycle bike-share system. BCycle runs the actual bikes and docking stations on the street; this site is an independent viewer on top of the public data they publish. It does three things the official app doesn't lean into:

  • Right now — how many bikes and open docks are at every station this minute.
  • Over time — what a station, or the whole system, usually looks like at this hour, so you can tell "busy for a Tuesday morning" from "normal."
  • Movement — an animated replay of where bikes have been going over the last day.

You don't need an account and nothing here rents you a bike — for that you still use the BCycle app. This is the map you check before you walk to a station, to make sure a bike (or an open dock) will be there when you arrive.

The four screens

Everything lives behind four tabs in the top bar: Live, Route, Flow, and Explore. The rest of this guide walks each one. There's also a light/dark toggle and an About button (the ⓘ icon) in the top bar.

2. Words you need first

A handful of terms show up everywhere. Read these once and the rest of the guide reads easily.

Station
A physical docking rack on the street. Bikes are locked into docks. On the map, a station is one teardrop-shaped pin.
Bikes vs. docks
Two numbers that always move in opposite directions. Bikes available = what you can ride away. Open docks = empty spots to return a bike. A full station has lots of bikes and zero open docks; an empty one is the reverse. Both matter: bikes if you're starting a trip, docks if you're ending one.
Capacity
Bikes + open docks = the total size of a station. A 25-dock station is bigger than a 6-dock one, and the pin is drawn larger to match.
Live / staleness
"Live" data is the most recent reading, refreshed every few minutes. A small pulsing green dot marks live numbers. If the feed goes quiet, the app tells you how old the data is rather than pretending it's current — see Good to know.
Typical
What a station usually has at this hour and day of week, learned from weeks of history. Used to tell you whether right now is above or below normal.
Corridor
A named group of nearby stations (a neighborhood, or a compass sector like "north"). Used to filter the map down to one part of town.
Trip
One bike's journey from one station to another. The app infers these by watching counts change (it can't see riders directly) — explained in section 11.

3. How to read a pin

The pin is the whole map in miniature, so it's worth thirty seconds. Every pin carries four pieces of information at once: two numbers, a size, a color, and (optionally) a colored ring.

Tap any pin to open its popup: the station name, a link to it in Google Maps, the live bikes/docks, what kinds of bikes are there, how long ago the data was read, and a little sparkline of bikes over the last day. From the popup, "Open details →" takes you to the full station page (section 6).

4. The colored ring

Some pins have a thin colored ring around them. The ring is the single most useful feature for planning, so it gets its own section. It answers: is this station better or worse stocked than usual right now?

Why this is useful: a station with 2 bikes sounds low, but if it's usually empty at 8am, that's just Tuesday. The ring compares against that station's own history, so a green ring means "stocked better than this station normally is," and amber means "running lower than normal — don't count on it." A station that's behaving normally gets no ring, which keeps the map calm and makes the rings that do appear worth noticing.

Rings need a little history to appear. A station needs at least a few days of data before any ring shows, and a few weeks before the comparison gets specific to each day of the week. Brand-new stations won't have rings until they've been watched for a while. You can hide the rings entirely from the map if you prefer a plain view.

5. The Live map Live tab

This is the home screen: the whole system on one map, every station a pin, updated continuously. Pan and zoom like any map. The map fits itself to the city's stations the first time it loads.

What's on screen

  • Pins for every station, drawn as described in section 3.
  • A totals card showing the system-wide bikes, docks, and capacity right now, plus how long ago that changed.
  • Filter chips at the top (a gear button on mobile opens the same controls).

Filtering the map

  • Minimum bikes — hide stations below a threshold (Any, 1+, 5+, 10+, 15+). Useful when you only care where you can actually grab a bike.
  • Corridor — narrow to one neighborhood or sector (only appears when the city has more than one corridor).

Filters are saved in the page address, so a filtered view survives a refresh and can be shared by copying the URL. A pin briefly pulses when its bike count changes, so you can catch activity as it happens.

6. Station details tap a pin → Open details

The deep-dive on a single station. Reach it from any pin's popup, from "nearby" lists, or directly by URL.

  • Right-now stats — current bikes and open docks, with the live green dot.
  • A mini map pinpointing the station, and a Google Maps link for walking directions.
  • Today's chart — bikes available over the last 24 hours, in the station's local time. Hover (or tap) any point for the exact reading.
  • Typical comparison — the same idea as the pin ring, spelled out in words: how now stacks up against this station's normal for this hour.
  • Activity — recent comings and goings at this station, and trips inferred to or from it.
  • Top routes — the most common destinations from here and origins to here, with how long the ride usually takes.
  • Nearby stations — the closest few, with their live counts and a link each, for when your first choice is empty.

7. Route check Route tab

Planning a specific A→B trip. Pick a start and a destination from the two dropdowns (the swap button reverses them). The page then answers the two questions that actually matter for a bike-share trip:

  • Will there be a bike where I'm starting? — the start station's bikes over the day.
  • Will there be an open dock where I'm going? — the destination's open docks over the day. (A trip you can't end is as bad as one you can't start.)

You also get an estimated ride time and distance, a map of the approximate route, and — if enough real trips have been observed — an average actual duration ("avg 14 min over 23 trips"). Hovering one chart shifts a guide line on the other by the travel time, so you can line up "leave now" against "arrive then." Copy link to this route shares the exact pair.

8. Flow Flow tab

A replay, not a planner. Flow animates the trips inferred over the last 24 hours as dots moving between stations, so you can watch the city's bike movement as a time-lapse.

  • Play / pause with the button or the spacebar.
  • Scrub the timeline to jump to any moment; the caption shows how many trips are in flight.
  • Fog (top-left toggle) darkens the map and only reveals the paths bikes have actually traveled, so popular corridors light up over the replay. It's off by default — flip it on for the prettier, more revealing view.
Flow shows inferred trips (section 11), packed so there's always something moving. Treat it as an impression of where bikes go, not an exact ledger of every ride.

9. Explore & Activity Explore tab

The patterns view, for the whole system rather than one station.

  • Active riders over time — roughly how many bikes are out (not parked) across the week.
  • Hour-of-week heatmaps — a 7×24 grid of when bikes are out riding vs. sitting in docks. The dark squares are your rush hours.
  • Travel-time matrix — a grid of estimated ride times between every pair of stations. Pick an origin and destination to highlight, or click any cell to jump straight into a Route check for that pair.
  • Activity log — a preview of recent events, with a "View all" link to the full Activity page (every recent departure, arrival, and inferred trip). Click a trip to see its route on a map.
Some panels say "coming soon" until enough history has piled up — the weekly heatmaps, for instance, need about a week of readings before they're meaningful. They fill in on their own.

10. Where the data comes from

Understanding the plumbing explains a few things you'll notice, like why numbers update on a delay and why history takes time to appear.

Two consequences worth knowing:

  • The map is a few minutes behind reality. Readings land every five minutes, so a bike grabbed thirty seconds ago may still show. That's normal, and the app tells you the reading's age rather than hiding it.
  • History accumulates. Typical comparisons, the weekly heatmaps, and trip stats all get better the longer the system has been watched. A freshly added city or station starts sparse and fills in.

11. How trips are figured out

The feed never says "rider X rode from A to B." It only reports how many bikes sit at each station. So trips are inferred: when a bike disappears from one station and one appears at another a sensible ride-time later, the app pairs them into a likely trip.

Quiet periods are easy — one bike out, one bike in, obvious pair. Busy periods are harder, so the app leans on the travel-time matrix (the same ride-time estimates from Route check) to match each arrival to the departure whose timing fits best. It's a best guess, kept to a recent rolling window, which is why Flow and the activity log are labeled as inferred. They're directionally honest, not a receipt for every ride.

12. Good to know

The numbers stopped updating — is it broken?
If a reading is more than a few minutes old you'll see an amber "X min ago" note; past ten minutes, a red banner warns the feed looks stale. Usually it's a brief hiccup on the source feed and it catches up on its own.
A station is grey. What does that mean?
It's offline — not currently renting or accepting returns (maintenance, full lockout, etc.). Its numbers may be there but you can't rely on using it.
Why does a station have no ring?
Either it's behaving about normally for this hour, or it doesn't have enough history yet for a confident comparison (new stations, or a newly added city).
Can I share what I'm looking at?
Yes. Filters, a selected station, and a route pair all live in the page address, so copying the URL shares your exact view. Route check has a dedicated copy button.
Is this the official BCycle app?
No. It's an independent viewer built on BCycle's public data. To actually rent a bike, use BCycle's own app — there's a link in the About dialog.
Does it track me?
There's no login and no personal data. The app keeps anonymous, aggregate usage counts (which pages get viewed) to guide what to improve — nothing tied to you.
